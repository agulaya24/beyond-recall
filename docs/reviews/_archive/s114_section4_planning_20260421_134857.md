### Critical issues in §1-§3

1.  **Mismatch between primary claim and statistical evidence (§1.3).** The paper claims the primary result is a **gradient**, which is a correlational relationship. However, the only statistic offered is a Wilcoxon signed-rank test (p=0.0063), which tests for a difference in medians between two paired groups (C5 vs. C4a). This test supports the claim that the specification *improves* scores, but it does **not** support the claim that the improvement is a *gradient* dependent on the baseline. To support the gradient claim, §4 must report the statistics for the linear regression itself: the p-value for the slope coefficient and the R-squared value. Without this, the central claim of the paper is statistically unsubstantiated by the evidence presented in the introduction.

2.  **Result presented in Related Work (§2.3).** The comparison to the Twin-2K benchmark includes a specific result: "we obtained 71.83% accuracy at 18:1 compression (p=0.008)." This is a finding from the author's own research. It does not belong in the Related Work section. It should be moved to §4, likely in a section on robustness or external validity, to avoid confusing the reader about what is prior art versus a novel contribution of this paper.

3.  **Overstating the reliability of the judge panel (§3.7.4).** The Krippendorff's α of 0.535 is only "moderate" agreement on absolute scores. The paper correctly pivots to the high Spearman ρ (rank agreement) to support its directional claims. However, this weakness in absolute agreement must be carried forward into §4. Any claims resting on the precise magnitude of a score (e.g., "a mean gain of +1.04 points") must be framed with the caveat that absolute judge agreement is not high. The paper's "cross-anchor interpretation rule" (§3.7.3) is an excellent way to do this, and it should be used consistently as the primary lens for interpreting effect size.

4.  **Inclusion of a poorly calibrated judge in the primary aggregate (§3.7.2).** The calibration data shows Gemini Pro is a highly unreliable instrument, severely penalizing length and failing to recognize verbatim matches consistently. Including it in the primary 7-judge aggregate risks contaminating the results. The paper's plan to report both 7-judge and 5-judge (non-Gemini) aggregates is good, but for credibility, **the 5-judge panel should be presented as the primary result in §4**, with the 7-judge panel reported as a sensitivity check. Leading with a known-flawed aggregate undermines the rigor of the analysis.

### Critical Issues in §1-3

1. **Overclaiming the Gradient’s Generalizability (§1.3, §1.4)**
   - **Claim**: "Nearly every real AI user is a low-baseline case by construction, which makes the low-baseline slice of our results the population of relevance."
   - **Issue**: The paper extrapolates from 14 public-domain autobiographies (all high-pretraining figures) to "nearly every real AI user" without testing living users. The gradient’s slope is derived from a biased sample (historical figures whose writing is in training corpora), and the low-baseline slice (C5 ≤ 2.0) is still higher than the *true* baseline for living users (C5 ≤ 1.0). This risks overstating the specification’s real-world utility.
   - **Fix**: Reframe the gradient as a *hypothesis* about living users, not a proven effect. Add a disclaimer that the study’s "low-baseline" slice is still higher than the expected baseline for private individuals, and that direct measurement is needed.

2. **Circularity in Wrong-Spec Control (§1.3, §3.5)**
   - **Claim**: "The improvement comes from the right content for the right person. Any structured prompt is not enough; only the correct specification for the correct subject produces the effect."
   - **Issue**: The wrong-spec control (C2c) uses named specifications (e.g., Franklin’s spec applied to Keckley), which allows the model to detect mismatches via trivial name/era/culture cues (e.g., "16th-century Central Asian military ruler"). This conflates *content mismatch* with *identity mismatch*, undermining the claim that the effect is content-specific.
   - **Fix**: Run a name-blind wrong-spec control (e.g., anonymize all specs) to isolate content-driven effects. Alternatively, acknowledge that the current control tests *identity mismatch* rather than *content mismatch*.

3. **Hedging as a Confound (§1.3, §3.6)**
   - **Claim**: "The specification shifts response models in the opposite direction: baseline hedging of 25.0% drops to 2.6% with the specification alone."
   - **Issue**: The rubric treats epistemically honest refusals (e.g., "I cannot answer without the specific passage") as "wholly wrong" (score = 1), while speculative answers (even if wrong) score higher. This penalizes the specification for *reducing* hedging, creating a false trade-off between honesty and accuracy. The paper frames this as a feature ("measurable feature of specification-based reasoning"), but it’s a methodological flaw: the rubric doesn’t distinguish between *refusal to fabricate* and *failure to predict*.
   - **Fix**: Split the rubric into two dimensions: (1) *predictive accuracy* (1-5) and (2) *epistemic honesty* (binary: hedged/refused vs. committed). Report both separately. Alternatively, acknowledge that the current rubric conflates these dimensions and may underestimate the specification’s value.

4. **Memory System Comparisons Lack Fair Baselines (§2.1, §3.5)**
   - **Claim**: "The specification layers additively on three of four commercial memory systems."
   - **Issue**: The paper compares the specification’s effect on memory systems *without controlling for retrieval quality*. Supermemory’s near-zero delta could reflect its superior standalone retrieval (C1 mean ~2.65) rather than incompatibility with the specification. The "controlled" condition (identical fact pool) doesn’t account for how each system’s retrieval *ranks* facts (e.g., Supermemory may retrieve more relevant facts, leaving less room for the spec to improve).
   - **Fix**: Report the specification’s effect *relative to each system’s retrieval ceiling* (e.g., delta = C3 - C1). Alternatively, acknowledge that the current comparison conflates retrieval quality with specification compatibility.

5. **LLM-as-Judge Limitations Undermine Key Claims (§3.7)**
   - **Claim**: "The specification improves representational accuracy."
   - **Issue**: The paper relies on LLM judges to score responses, but the rubric’s anchors are subjective (e.g., "right direction with specifics" vs. "right domain, no specifics"). The calibration data shows judges disagree on absolute scores (Krippendorff α = 0.535), and the Gemini judges inflate scores by ~1 point. While Spearman ρ = 0.89-0.98 suggests directional agreement, this doesn’t validate the *magnitude* of the specification’s effect.
   - **Fix**: Add a human-annotated subset (e.g., 10% of responses) to validate the LLM judges’ scoring. Alternatively, weaken claims about the *size* of the effect (e.g., "the specification shifts responses in the right direction" rather than "improves accuracy by X points").
