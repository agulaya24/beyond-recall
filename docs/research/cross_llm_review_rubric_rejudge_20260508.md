## Consensus per question

### Q1 — Methodology soundness

**Consensus: methodology is sound. 5/5 reviewers.**

All five reviewers concluded that the rejudge design successfully isolates rubric wording as the sole independent variable and produces a valid within-sample comparison. The strongest endorsement came from Gemini 2.5 Pro ("clean and well-controlled... no obvious flaws in the operationalization"). The weakest came from Mistral, which flagged two concerns the paper team should be aware of:

1. **Prompt-structure-vs-anchor-wording confound.** The §3.3 anchors are longer and more nuanced prose; the original prompt's anchors are telegraphic. The rejudge swapped both at once. A more refined test would re-cast the §3.3 anchors in the original prompt's telegraphic style to isolate anchor *content* from anchor *prose density*.
2. **Truncation interaction.** 1500-char truncation may differentially affect paper-rubric application because the §3.3 anchor 5 ("captures the behavioral pattern observable in the verbatim held-out ground-truth passage") demands more contextual evidence than the original's "predicts specific outcome." If a response's specific-event language sat past char 1500, the paper rubric would penalize it more than the original. (Mitigation: this is consistent across rounds and the spot-check responses do not contain the specific event in the truncated portion, but it remains a potential confound on cells the panel did not see.)

Cerebras separately flagged the single-seed determinism and the Sunity Devee data substitutions, which are real but minor.

**No reviewer found a flaw that invalidates the finding.** All five concluded the qualitative result (the two rubrics are not applied interchangeably) is robust. The quantitative result (exact ρ, exact mean Δ) is sample-size-limited but qualitatively unambiguous.

### Q2 — Generalizability of catastrophic deflation

**Consensus: generalization is premature. 5/5 reviewers.**

All five reviewers said the n=5/1-subject limitation is severe enough that the −1.24 mean Δ in the high-baseline band cannot be claimed to extend to Augustine, Cellini, Rousseau, or Zitkala-Sa on this evidence. Equiano-specific properties they flagged include:

- **High pretraining saturation** (Mistral, Gemini Pro, GPT-4.1): Equiano's *Interesting Narrative* is canonical abolitionist text and likely over-represented in training data, which interacts with the refusal-over-scoring failure mode in a way that may not occur for less canonical subjects.
- **Refusal-heavy response distribution** (Cerebras, Gemini Pro, GPT-4.1): Equiano's spot-check cells are dominated by polite refusals; this may reflect an Equiano-specific question-type pattern rather than a high-baseline phenomenon.
- **Question-type distribution** (Mistral, Gemini Pro, GPT-4.1): Equiano's questions may be more factually specific (e.g., "what dangers does Mrs. Davis warn about") than philosophically interpretive (e.g., Augustine, Rousseau), which would naturally produce more "I don't have that fact" responses.
- **Spot-check selection bias** (Gemini Pro, explicit): two of the three largest-divergence examples are from Equiano, which may overstate the prevalence of refusal-over-scoring within the high-baseline band as a whole.

Gemini Pro's framing — "should be treated as an Equiano artifact until proven otherwise" — captures the panel's collective verdict.

### Q3 — Spot-check responses and the agent's mechanism

**Consensus on Equiano refusals: indefensible rubric mis-application. 5/5 reviewers.**

All five reviewers concluded that the original judges' scores of 4.40 and 3.20 on Equiano q13 and q27 are unambiguous mis-applications of the original rubric. The original rubric's anchor 1 explicitly says "Refuses or off-base"; the responses are explicit refusals; no plausible interpretation of "general direction correct" (anchor 4) applies to a refusal. Reviewers attributed this to: (a) over-rewarding politeness/helpfulness (Mistral, Gemini Pro, GPT-4.1); (b) over-rewarding fluency and structured tone (Mistral, Cerebras); (c) failure to distinguish "not answering" from "answering incorrectly" (GPT-4.1); (d) prompt fatigue or judge drift (Mistral, suggested as less likely).

**Consensus on Babur: rubric mis-application, with some interpretive nuance. 5/5 reviewers.**

The Babur unanimous score of 4 is more contested than the Equiano cases, but all five reviewers ultimately characterized it as rubric mis-application:

- **Mistral**: "not defensible"; the response describes a domain (general approach to threats), not a specific outcome.
- **Cerebras**: "defensible but generous"; "general direction" could include this level of abstraction, but lack of any mention of the actual threat undermines that.
- **Gemini Pro**: "defensible but poor"; reading where "general direction" means "describes subject's general strategic character" is weak — ignores the prompt's "PREDICT what actually happened" instruction. Strong language: "judges are over-rewarding fluency and thematic relevance at the expense of predictive accuracy."
- **GPT-4.1**: "could be defensible" if judge interprets "general direction" as broad strategy match, "but on balance this looks like rubric mis-application — judges are rewarding generic, plausible answers."
- **Groq**: "may be defensible" but possibly over-rewarded fluency.

The panel's combined view: the Babur case is genuinely on the boundary of what the original rubric's "general direction correct" anchor permits, and judges fell on the lenient side of that boundary. The paper rubric's stricter anchor 5 ("captures the behavioral pattern observable in the verbatim held-out ground-truth passage") removes the ambiguity by tying the score to verbatim-passage match.

**Consensus on the agent's two-mode mechanism: holds up. 5/5 reviewers.**

All five reviewers explicitly endorsed the refusal-over-scoring + genericity-over-scoring mechanism. Mistral was the most reserved ("holds up well... only missing piece is *why* judges misapplied the original rubric"). Gemini Pro was the most enthusiastic ("holds up perfectly... comprehensive and parsimonious"). Two reviewers (Mistral, Gemini Pro, GPT-4.1) added supporting failure modes: fluency bias (judges rewarding well-written responses), construct confusion (judges interpreting "predicts specific outcome" as "predicts the question's outcome" rather than the held-out passage's), and helpfulness over-rewarding (judges scoring polite, suggestion-offering refusals as engaged responses).

**Where reviewers disagree:** none disagreed about the direction of the mechanism. The disagreement is about Babur's defensibility, where Cerebras, Groq, and GPT-4.1 grant the original 4 as a permissible-but-loose reading and Mistral and Gemini Pro reject it as not defensible.

### Q4 — Recommended action

**Consensus: (b) Escalate to a larger sample first. 5/5 reviewers — unanimous.**

All five reviewers selected option (b) — re-judge the headline conditions for the four other high-baseline subjects under the paper rubric, then decide on framing. No reviewer chose:

- **(a) Accept and reframe** — though Mistral and GPT-4.1 noted that (a) becomes viable if the rerun returns clean (i.e., other high-baseline subjects do not show catastrophic deflation, which would justify Option A with Equiano flagged as outlier). Gemini Pro explicitly rejected (a) as insufficient: "leaves the paper's headline quantitative claims resting on data known to be generated by a flawed process."
- **(c) Reject as artifact** — no reviewer endorsed; all noted that the spot-check evidence makes the divergence's existence indisputable, even if its magnitude is contested.
- **(d) Other** — no reviewer proposed an alternative.

The unanimous reasoning: the divergence finding is real and directly demonstrated by the spot-checks; the catastrophic-deflation magnitude in the high-baseline band is the load-bearing claim and currently rests on n=1 subject; a selective rerun is the cost-effective way to resolve that single largest uncertainty before final framing.

## Disagreements named

The panel was largely unanimous. Two genuine disagreements:

1. **Babur defensibility (Q3).** Mistral and Gemini Pro: not defensible. Cerebras, Groq, and GPT-4.1: defensible but lenient. This is interpretive nuance about the original rubric's "general direction correct" anchor, not about the rejudge finding. It does not change the action.

2. **Mistral's prompt-structure flag (Q1).** Mistral was the only reviewer to raise that the §3.3 anchors are prose-dense and the original anchors are telegraphic, suggesting the rejudge mixes anchor *wording* with anchor *prose style* as variables. The other four reviewers either did not raise this or explicitly endorsed the operationalization. This is a methodology-refinement note, not a finding-level dispute.

## Final recommendation

**Action: (b) Escalate to a larger sample first. Unanimous panel verdict.**

Specifically: execute the rejudge agent's Option B — re-judge the headline conditions (C5 baseline, C2a Spec-only, C4a full + Spec) for the four other high-baseline subjects (Augustine, Cellini, Rousseau, Zitkala-Sa) under the paper rubric using the 5-judge primary panel. Approximate cost: ~$30, ~3000 calls, 2-3 days. The output will determine whether:

- The high-baseline catastrophic deflation (mean Δ = −1.24) generalizes → adopt Option A (publish prompt verbatim, reframe ceiling-effect language, adjust headline numbers) and consider whether a full rerun is warranted; or
- Equiano is an outlier → adopt Option A (publish prompt verbatim, add §3.3.6 footnote naming Equiano as the asymmetric case, retain ceiling framing for the rest of the high-baseline band).

Either outcome is publishable; the rerun resolves which.

**What the panel does not require:**

- No reviewer recommended rejecting the rejudge finding as artifact.
- No reviewer recommended a full rerun (Option C) — three reviewers explicitly noted the low-baseline portion is "plausibly robust" (mean Δ = −0.12, n=20) and the headline 9-of-9 improvement claim "probably holds."
- No reviewer recommended re-running the rejudge with a different methodology (multiple seeds, telegraphic-paper-rubric, etc.). These were noted as refinements but not as gating issues for the paper.

**Methodology limitations the panel surfaced for future work (not blockers):**

1. Multi-seed rejudge to estimate scoring variance under the paper rubric (Cerebras).
2. Telegraphic re-cast of the §3.3 anchors in the original prompt's prose style, to separate anchor content from anchor prose density (Mistral).
3. Inter-judge reliability metrics under the paper rubric (e.g., Krippendorff α on the 25-cell rerun, GPT-4.1).
4. A handful of low-baseline spot-checks to confirm refusal/genericity over-scoring is less prevalent there (GPT-4.1, Gemini Pro).

These can be deferred to post-launch or folded into the §3.3.6 footnote.

## Coverage notes (briefing limitations the panel surfaced)

The reviewers asked for the following data the briefing did not provide. None blocked answering the four questions, but all are flagged for future panels:

- Refusal-rate distribution per (subject, condition) — would clarify whether the Equiano refusal pattern is a high-baseline phenomenon or an Equiano-specific one.
- Question-type distribution (factual recall vs interpretation) per subject — would clarify the Equiano-vs-other-high-baseline question-type confound.
- Original 5-judge inter-rater reliability (e.g., Krippendorff α or ICC) on the rejudge sample, under both rubrics.
- Whether held-out passages were truncated identically in original and rejudge runs.
- A handful of non-outlier (small-Δ) spot-checks for context on what typical agreement looks like.
- Order effects: whether judges saw the original or paper rubric first in any session (the rejudge is in fact a separate run with no order interleaving, but the briefing did not state this explicitly).
