# §4.4.1 insertion: cross-system retrieval overlap

Status: draft for §4.4.1, after the controlled-vs-native aggregate table.
Generated 2026-05-01 from `scripts/analyze_retrieval_overlap.py`.
Data: `docs/research/retrieval_overlap_analysis_20260501.json`.
Purpose: answer the §1.2 promise. Given identical input, do memory-system providers converge on which facts are most relevant?

---

Providers do not converge. Given the same all-facts pool and the same 546 behavioral-prediction questions across the 14 main-study subjects, the five systems retrieve different top-K facts for the same question. Mean pairwise Jaccard across the ten system pairs in the controlled retrieval configuration is 0.083 raw and 0.088 after lowercase-and-whitespace normalization. With ten facts retrieved per system per question, that is roughly one to two shared facts out of an average union of seventeen. The shared-fact pool is identical; the rankings over it are not. Same input, different top-10s.

The pair structure is consistent. BaseLayer and Supermemory share the most (mean Jaccard 0.146), followed by Mem0–Letta (0.126) and BaseLayer–Mem0 (0.123). Pairs involving Zep are the lowest (Supermemory–Zep 0.025, Letta–Zep 0.026, BaseLayer–Zep 0.027). The Zep gap reflects ranking method: Zep returns a serialized graph-edge blob from which we extract top-level `fact='...'` kwargs (yielding 9.8 unique facts on average), and graph-traversal scoring overlaps weakly with the embedding-similarity rankings the other systems use. Letta's controlled retrieval emits 10 entries per question but only 3.5 unique facts on average; the same fact recurs under graph traversal. We compute Jaccard on unique sets, so duplication does not directly inflate overlap, but it does compress Letta's effective retrieval depth.

This ties to the §4.4.2 patterns. The three downstream patterns that distinguish memory-system behavior (interpretive supply, over-theorization, and spec-induced refusal) emerge in part from this retrieval divergence. Providers do not disagree about which facts exist; the all-facts pool is shared. They disagree about which facts the question is asking for. Same question, different evidentiary substrates, different downstream stories. Per-subject Jaccard varies from 0.043 (Equiano) to 0.115 (Hamerton); per-category variation is small (0.076–0.093 across decisions, values, relationships, conflict, learning). The divergence is a property of provider ranking, not of question type or subject. Normalization shifts mean Jaccard by less than one point; the divergence is structural, not punctuation.

For the §1.2 question, the controlled answer is no: the provider layer does not converge on relevance. Each retrieval algorithm encodes its own theory of what counts as relevant, and those theories produce nearly disjoint top-10s over the same ground truth. The native pipeline points the same direction more starkly (mean Jaccard 0.000 across the four native systems, six pairs) but is not directly interpretable, since native retrievals return heterogeneous objects (Mem0 third-person summaries, Letta raw passages, Supermemory atomic facts, Zep graph rows). The controlled configuration is the cleanest test, and it isolates the ranking layer as a load-bearing source of representational accuracy: which facts the system surfaces determines, before any reading model engages, what the response can be about.
