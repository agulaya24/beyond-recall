# Stats Update — Wilcoxon Suite + Krippendorff's Alpha

_Generated by `scripts/stats_wilcoxon_krippendorff_update.py`._

## Method

- **Primary panel:** Haiku 4.5, Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.6, GPT-4o, GPT-5.4 (5 judges). Used for every Wilcoxon test.
- **7-judge panel:** primary + Gemini Flash + Gemini Pro. Used only for Krippendorff's alpha, per the task spec. Gemini Pro covers 4/14 subjects (hamerton, augustine, babur, bernal_diaz); for the other 10 subjects the alpha reduces to the 6 available judges at the per-question level. This is handled naturally by the pair-count formula (units with m coders contribute m*(m-1) pairs) and the lack of uniform Pro coverage is not an averaging artifact.
- **Aggregation for Wilcoxon:** per (subject, condition, judge) mean across questions; per (subject, condition) mean across judges in the 5-judge panel; subject is the unit of inference.
- **Wilcoxon:** `scipy.stats.wilcoxon` two-sided, `zero_method='wilcox'` (drops zeros). N is the number of paired subjects; effective N after dropping zero-difference pairs is also reported.
- **Bootstrap CI:** 10,000 resamples of the paired-difference vector with `numpy.random.default_rng(seed=42)`; percentile 2.5/97.5 on the resampled median. Reported as a CI on the median paired difference.
- **Krippendorff's alpha (interval):** pair-count formula. Unit = `(subject, condition, question_id)`. D_o uses sum over units of (1/(m_u-1)) * sum_{i != j} (v_i - v_j)^2; D_e uses (2N * sum(v^2) - 2 * sum(v)^2) / (N*(N-1)) over the full value pool. Sanity-checked: perfect agreement -> alpha=1.0; +1 systematic bias -> 0 < alpha < 1.

## 4. Krippendorff's alpha (interval scale)

Alpha is computed on raw per-question scores, not on subject-level aggregates. Unit = (subject, condition, question_id). Missing judge observations are handled by the pair-count formula: a unit with m coders contributes m*(m-1) ordered pairs to D_o and the expected disagreement D_e is estimated over the full value pool.

**Interpretive thresholds (Krippendorff 2004):** α ≥ 0.667 acceptable for exploratory work; α ≥ 0.8 good; α < 0.667 flagged.

| Condition | 7-judge α | N units (7j) | 5-judge α | N units (5j) | Threshold |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| C5_baseline | 0.6830 | 546 | 0.7585 | 546 | acceptable (0.667 - 0.8) |
| C2a_full_spec | 0.3825 | 546 | 0.5438 | 546 | **BELOW 0.667** |
| C4a_full_facts_plus_spec | 0.3874 | 546 | 0.5683 | 546 | **BELOW 0.667** |
| Across-condition (pooled) | 0.5221 | 2,730 | 0.6543 | 2,730 | **BELOW 0.667** |

## 5. Comparison vs the v9 Spearman ρ = 0.89-0.98 claim

The v9 paper §3.7.4 reports pairwise Spearman ρ = 0.89 to 0.98 across all 21 judge pairs (7 judges) and frames this as rank agreement on direction. The paper also reports Krippendorff α = 0.659 (5-judge ordinal) and α = 0.535 (7-judge ordinal).

Krippendorff's α as computed here is **interval scale** (the task asked for interval). The v9-reported α is **ordinal**. Interval α is stricter than ordinal α on score data that has natural ordering but doesn't behave linearly at the anchors; expect interval α to be lower. They are not directly comparable as a single number, but they answer the same question: absolute agreement on raw per-question scores.

Computed Spearman ρ ranges (this recompute, 21 judge pairs on condition-level aggregates — per (subject, condition) mean per judge):

- **5-judge primary (10 pairs):** range [0.858, 0.932], mean 0.898
- **7-judge full panel (21 pairs):** range [0.294, 0.932], mean 0.734

**The 5-judge primary range [0.858, 0.932] differs from the v9-cited range [0.89, 0.98].** The upper bound of 0.98 does not appear in any aggregation attempted here (per-question, per-(subject,condition), per-subject, or within-condition); the highest pairwise ρ computed on the 5-judge primary panel is 0.932 (sonnet × opus). The v9 range may have been computed on a different unit (e.g., condition-level means pooled across subjects, or an aggregation that includes within-judge replicates). **Flag for v9 §3.7.4:** recheck the provenance of the 0.89-0.98 range; the computed range using the standard per-(subject,condition) aggregation is [0.858, 0.932] on the 5-judge panel and [0.294, 0.932] on the 7-judge panel (the low end of the 7-judge range is the gemini_pro × sonnet pair, pulled down by Gemini Pro's partial coverage and +1 absolute bias).

Krippendorff α (interval, this recompute) compared to v9-reported α (ordinal):

| Panel | v9 α (ordinal) | Recompute α (interval, pooled) |
|---|---:|---:|
| 5-judge primary | 0.659 | 0.6543 |
| 7-judge | 0.535 | 0.5221 |

Ordering matches (5-judge > 7-judge in both versions), which corroborates the v9 framing that Gemini's +1 absolute bias drops absolute-agreement α even though it doesn't affect rank ρ. The near-equal values (interval within 0.005-0.013 of ordinal) should be read as coincidence, not validation. Ordinal and interval α diverge significantly on distributions with heavy clustering at rubric anchors (e.g., many 1s or 5s with few middle values); our distribution happens to produce similar numbers under both. This is not evidence that either formulation is "correct" — it just means on this particular score distribution, the two measures happen to converge.

- **§3.7.4 Spearman ρ range:** v9 cites 0.89-0.98 across 21 judge pairs. Recompute on the 5-judge primary panel (10 pairs) gives [0.86, 0.93]; the 7-judge panel (21 pairs) gives [0.29, 0.93]. Recommend: either find the original aggregation that produced 0.98 (possibly condition-level marginals with fewer cells), or update the cited range to [0.86, 0.93] (5-judge, 10 pairs) — which preserves the paper's directional claim without the unverified 0.98.

Ordinal and interval are different measures; the v9 values remain correct if computed on the ordinal scale. No change to v9 required unless the paper intends to cite both.
