## Drop-in paragraph (to insert after Figure 4.1, before "**Per-subject results.**")

**Reading the gradient.** The x-axis of Figure 4.1 is the no-context baseline score (C5) for each subject on the 1-5 rubric. The y-axis is the per-subject lift the specification produces over that baseline (Δ_C4a, the C4a facts+spec score minus the C5 baseline score). Each point is one of the 14 historical subjects. The 9 low-baseline subjects (C5 ≤ 2.0, the population of relevance from §3.2.1) cluster in the upper-left quadrant, all with positive Δ, ranging from Babur at +0.25 (smallest lift, baseline 1.76) to Hamerton at +1.51 (largest lift, baseline 1.26). Franklin sits in the lower-right at C5 = 3.77, Δ = −0.13, the high-baseline reference point where the model already knows the subject from pretraining and the specification adds nothing. The regression line falls from upper-left to lower-right with slope −0.96 and R² = 0.82, which reads as: the lower the model's pretraining baseline on a subject, the larger the lift the specification produces, and that single relationship explains 82% of the variance in spec-effect across subjects. Above the regression line and worth naming: Sunity Devee (baseline 1.03, Δ +1.38) and Hamerton (baseline 1.26, Δ +1.51) outperform the linear fit; Babur (baseline 1.76, Δ +0.25) underperforms it as the low-baseline outlier. The takeaway: the specification helps most where the model knows the subject least, and once a subject crosses into the high-baseline band the specification has no representational gap to fill.
