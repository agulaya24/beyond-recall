# Hamerton confound note (2026-04-28)

## Context

Stream X (`big_wins_characterization_20260428.md`) reported that Hamerton accounts for 25% of unique extreme upward jumps (15 of 60) and attributed this to "Hamerton has the long unified-brief vs. globals' six-section spec."

That attribution is empirically wrong. The spec-length direction is inverted.

## Verified spec-length facts

What gets served at C2a (spec only) and as the spec component of C4a (facts + spec):

| Subject | File served | Word count |
|---|---|---:|
| Hamerton | `data/hamerton/spec/brief_v5_clean.md` | 1,918 |
| Equiano (global) | `data/global_subjects/equiano/spec_production.md` | (similar to Ebers) |
| Ebers (global, illustrative) | `data/global_subjects/ebers/spec_production.md` | 5,775 |

Average global `spec_production.md` ≈ 5,775 words. Hamerton's served spec is 1,918 words.

**Hamerton's served spec is roughly 0.33x the size of the globals' served spec, not larger.**

The globals' `spec_production.md` is a multi-layer concatenation: anchors (epistemic axioms) + core (values/dispositions) + predictions (forward inferences) + brief (synthesizing layer). Hamerton's served spec is the unified brief only.

## Verified extreme-jump rate

Hamerton: 15 unique extreme jumps across 80-question battery = 18.75%
Other 13 subjects: 45 unique extreme jumps across 13 × 39 = 507 questions = 8.9%

**Hamerton's extreme-jump rate is 2.1x the average rate of the other 13 subjects.**

This elevation is real and not explained by spec length (which is inverse to jump rate).

## Candidate explanations the paper has not investigated

1. **Battery generator difference.** Hamerton's 80-question battery was generated by the legacy Haiku 4.5 prompt that originally produced Franklin and Hamerton. The 13 globals' batteries were regenerated by `run_global_rerun.py` against a uniform prompt template. The paper's existing Hamerton-leverage subset regression (drops Hamerton; slope attenuates 7%; gradient survives) checks the per-subject mean impact but does not check the per-question wins-rate impact. The legacy generator may have produced questions that are systematically more amenable to spec lift.

2. **Subject pretraining thinness.** Hamerton (Philip Gilbert Hamerton, 1834-1894, Victorian art critic and etcher) is plausibly less represented in pretraining corpora than the 13 globals (Augustine, Cellini, Rousseau, Babur, Bernal Diaz, etc., who are widely studied historical figures). Lower pretraining footprint = more room for spec to lift.

3. **Spec qualitative density.** Hamerton's brief-only format may pack more behavioral predicates per word than globals' anchors+core+predictions+brief stack. Anchors/core/predictions repeat each other and the brief synthesizes them. The brief may be the operational predicate-density maximum.

4. **Battery question-type composition.** Hamerton's 80-question battery may over-weight LITERAL_RECALL questions, which Stream X identified as the dominant axis on extreme jumps (2.77x panel rate). If Hamerton's battery happens to lean LITERAL_RECALL more than globals', the elevated jump rate is partly a battery-composition effect that the existing battery-question-type sensitivity analysis (Appendix B.6) controls for at the per-subject mean grain but not at the per-question wins grain.

## What this means for the framing

The framing-implications report should NOT treat Hamerton's elevated wins as evidence that "richer specifications produce more wins." The data does not support that. Hamerton has a smaller spec and more wins, which is a confound that needs explanation, not a finding that supports the spec-richness hypothesis.

The most defensible framing is: "Hamerton accounts for 25% of unique extreme jumps despite a served spec ~0.33x the size of globals' specs. The cause is not spec length. Candidate explanations include legacy battery generator effects, subject pretraining thinness, and behavioral-predicate density per word; these have not been disentangled in the present study and are flagged as future work."

The paper's existing Hamerton-leverage subset regression remains a valid sensitivity check at the per-subject mean grain. A parallel Hamerton-leverage check at the per-question wins grain (drop Hamerton's 15 extreme jumps, recompute the unique-wins rate on the 13 globals only) would tell us how much the wins-as-headline framing depends on Hamerton specifically.

## Recommended additions to Phase 3 framing report

1. Document the spec-length inversion explicitly so no downstream artifact carries forward Stream X's misattribution.
2. Compute the Hamerton-leverage check on the wins population (drop Hamerton's wins, see how the headline numbers shift).
3. Add a future-work bullet on disentangling battery-generator vs subject-pretraining-thinness vs predicate-density effects.
