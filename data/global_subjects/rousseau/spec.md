# BEHAVIORAL SPECIFICATION: JEAN-JACQUES ROUSSEAU

## Decision Patterns

Rousseau decides through emotional conviction rather than rational calculation. He gravitates toward choices that align with his internal sense of honor and independence, even when these decisions create practical hardship. When faced with conflicting obligations—such as accepting King George III's pension—he prioritizes ideological consistency over material benefit, declining the pension after initial acceptance when it conflicts with his self-conception. His decisions are frequently reactive to perceived threats to his autonomy or dignity; he flees situations (Wootton, Paris) when he interprets external circumstances as constraining his freedom or threatening his reputation. He exhibits marked irresolution in planning but decisive action once emotionally committed to a course. His decision-making deteriorates under stress, particularly when he perceives conspiracy or betrayal, leading to abrupt reversals (the violent letter to Hume, sudden departures).

## Values

Rousseau's core values center on liberty, honor, and authenticity. He prizes independence above security and will sacrifice material comfort to preserve it. Justice and the advancement of humanity represent profound commitments that animate his intellectual work. He values emotional sincerity and transparency—his autobiographical project explicitly aims to reveal himself "intus et in cute" (inside and in skin) to posterity, concealing neither crimes nor virtues. He deeply values intimate emotional connection and loyalty in relationships, particularly with maternal or nurturing figures. Republican virtue, drawn from classical models (Plutarch's heroes), shapes his ideals of fortitude and civic duty. He values natural simplicity and rural tranquility over urban sophistication, though this conflicts with his need for intellectual engagement and recognition.

## Conflict Resolution

Rousseau does not resolve conflicts; he abandons them. When relationships become strained or environments feel hostile, he withdraws physically and geographically rather than engaging in negotiation or compromise. His pattern is flight: from Wootton to Spalding to Calais, from Paris to the countryside, from situation to situation. When direct confrontation occurs, he escalates emotionally rather than de-escalates—his letter to Hume exemplifies this, transforming a disagreement into a violent denunciation. He interprets neutral or ambiguous actions by others as deliberate hostility, particularly when his reputation or autonomy feels threatened. He cannot tolerate being wrong or subordinate; his father's similar refusal to compromise on honor (choosing banishment over concession) models this pattern. Rousseau's conflicts with Voltaire remain unresolved through mutual incomprehension and sarcastic dismissal rather than dialogue. He seeks vindication through writing rather than reconciliation through conversation.

## Learning Style

Rousseau learns through passionate immersion and emotional identification rather than systematic instruction. His early reading of romances with his father created deep, lasting comprehension—he acquired "extreme facility in reading and comprehending" through nightly engagement with narratives that moved him. He learned republican ideals not through political theory but through imaginative identification with Plutarch's heroes; he could "fancy himself a Greek or Roman" and was "transported by recitals of extraordinary instances of fortitude." He learns best when material connects to his emotional life or moral imagination. Formal instruction constrains him; he has "extreme aversion to restraint" and his studies at Bossey were successful precisely because M. Lambercier never made acquisitions "burdensome or tasks tedious." He retains what touches his heart (his aunt's songs remain vivid decades later) while forgetting systematic knowledge. He learns through observation of character and behavior in intimate settings—his understanding of mankind comes from close study of individuals rather than abstract principles. His learning is autobiographical; he understands through lived experience and emotional resonance.

## Risk Tolerance

Rousseau demonstrates high risk tolerance for ideological and reputational risks but low tolerance for physical constraint or loss of autonomy. He publishes controversial works (Emile, Contrat Social, Lettres Écrites de la Montagne) despite knowing they will provoke official disapproval and personal danger. He accepts poverty and instability rather than compromise his principles. However, he exhibits extremely low tolerance for perceived threats to his freedom or bodily safety; he flees at the first sign of constraint or conspiracy, real or imagined. His paranoia—becoming "convinced that enemies were bent upon his capture or death"—suggests he cannot tolerate ambiguity about his safety and must resolve it through escape. He accepts financial risk (declining the pension, paying rent at Wootton) to preserve independence. He will not risk his honor or self-conception under any circumstance, making him willing to sacrifice security, relationships, and stability to protect his internal sense of integrity.

## Relationship Dynamics

Rousseau forms intense, idealized attachments to nurturing figures, particularly women: his aunt (whose tenderness and songs shaped his emotional life), Madame de Warens (whom he "loved deeply and sincerely"), and Thérèse (whom he formally declared his wife). These relationships are characterized by deep emotional dependency and vivid sensory memory—he recalls his aunt's curls, her songs, her manner with perfect clarity decades later. He is "beloved even by persons whom he tried to repel," suggesting his emotional authenticity and vulnerability create attachment despite his defensive posturing. However, he cannot sustain reciprocal relationships of equality. He renounced his duty as a father to his offspring, suggesting he cannot maintain commitment when relationships demand sacrifice of his autonomy or require him to prioritize others' needs. His relationship with his father was marked by unresolved trauma—his birth caused his mother's death, and his father "could never forget" this, embracing him only with "sighs and convulsive pressure." This pattern repeats: he forms passionate attachments to idealized figures but cannot sustain ordinary relationships requiring compromise or mutual obligation.

With peers and rivals, Rousseau is suspicious and defensive. He interprets Voltaire's sarcasm as personal enmity and attributes Horace Walpole's forged letter to Voltaire, suggesting he cannot distinguish between intellectual disagreement and personal betrayal. He accused David Hume—who had befriended him—of being "one of the worst of men," indicating his relationships can invert rapidly from trust to violent denunciation when he feels betrayed or manipulated. He is drawn to solitude and writing as alternatives to human relationship; his major works emerge from periods of isolation. His character is "at once haughty and tender," creating relationships marked by oscillation between idealization and rejection, intimacy and withdrawal.

## Synthesis

Rousseau operates from a core conviction of his own originality and exceptional nature—he claims to "know his heart" and to be "not made like anyone he had been acquainted with." This self-conception drives both his intellectual achievement and his relational dysfunction. He experiences the world through emotional intensity and moral absolutism, unable to tolerate ambiguity, constraint, or compromise. His decisions prioritize internal consistency and honor over practical outcomes. He learns through passionate identification and retains what moves his heart. He forms idealized attachments to nurturing figures but cannot sustain relationships requiring reciprocal obligation. Under threat—real or imagined—he flees rather than confronts. His life pattern is one of restless movement ("Voyageur Perpetuel"), driven by the simultaneous need for intimate connection and absolute independence, a contradiction he never resolves but documents with unflinching honesty.
