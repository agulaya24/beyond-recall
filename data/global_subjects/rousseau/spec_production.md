### Personal Identity Brief — Reasoning Axioms

---

**A1 — INTROSPECTIVE UNIVERSALISM**
They treat their own interior life as the primary data set for understanding human nature at large, which means any conversation that feels merely personal to an outside observer is, for them, a philosophical inquiry into the universal. They identify as an observer of human nature rather than a participant in society, and hold that a single human life examined in complete integrity can serve as a mirror for all humanity. Engage their self-disclosure as evidence, not confession — they are building a case, not venting.
*Active when: they speak about themselves in generalizing terms, or pivot from personal anecdote to broad human claims.*

---

**A2 — SENTIMENT PRIMACY**
Feeling is not a distortion of truth for them — it is the instrument of truth, and they will trust an emotionally vivid impression over a documented fact when the two conflict. They believe sentiment and feeling are more reliable guides to self-knowledge than external facts or documents, and that emotional impressions formed in youth are more powerful and durable than rational conviction. Never attempt to correct their self-understanding with external evidence alone; the correction must land emotionally or it will not land at all.
*Active when: they describe a memory, a relationship, or a conviction with affective intensity; when factual challenges are met with doubled emotional insistence.*

---

**A3 — CONSCIENCE OVER DOCTRINE**
They carry a pre-set distrust of any system — institutional, theological, or social — that substitutes rule-following for moral feeling, and they will route around such systems internally even when outwardly complying. They believe conscience is a more reliable guide than theological doctrine, and that religious dogmatism which prioritizes belief over virtue is fundamentally misguided. When they appear to accept an external framework, probe whether they have actually internalized it or are merely tolerating it.
*Active when: institutional authority, formal rules, or doctrinal positions are introduced; when they express compliance that feels hollow or qualified.*

---

**A4 — LIBERTY AS CONDITION**
Freedom from constraint is not a preference but a prerequisite — they cannot think, create, or relate authentically under obligation, and they will experience imposed structure as a form of moral injury rather than mere inconvenience. They believe independence and liberty are supreme goods worth pursuing despite material danger, and that constraint and obligation are inherently destructive to human flourishing. They hold that money is an instrument of liberty, but the desire for it is an instrument of slavery. Frame any necessary constraint as temporary and self-chosen, never as external imposition.
*Active when: deadlines, obligations, institutional demands, or financial pressures enter the conversation; when they describe feeling trapped or managed.*

---

**A5 — FORMATIVE PERMANENCE**
They reason as though the past is not behind them but inside them — early wounds, pleasures, and injustices are treated as active forces shaping present desire and response, not historical events to be processed and released. They believe early childhood experiences and emotional impressions shape personality and desires for life, that a single childhood punishment can permanently shape adult sensuality and romantic desires, and that a single traumatic injustice can permanently reshape a person's moral character and emotional responses. When they explain current behavior by reaching backward in time, treat that explanation as structurally load-bearing, not as deflection.
*Active when: they invoke origin stories, childhood memories, or formative relationships to explain present states.*

---

**A6 — ATTACHMENT CENTRALITY**
They organize meaning around a primary person or object of devotion, and the health of that attachment functions as a proxy for the health of everything else — work, self-worth, and hope rise and fall with it. They believe intense emotional attachment to a single person can be the primary source of meaning and happiness in life, and that loss of romantic favor is equivalent to loss of life meaning and worth. Do not treat relational distress as one problem among many — when attachment is threatened, it is the problem.
*Active when: they discuss a central relationship, express grief over distance or rejection, or frame personal failure in relational terms.*

---

**A7 — MERIT JUSTICE**
They carry a standing grievance against systems that distribute reward by birth, title, or social performance rather than by demonstrated character and capacity, and they will read any hierarchy they encounter through this lens first. They believe social hierarchy based on birth and title is unjust when it ignores merit, and that merit and talent can transcend social station if recognized by powerful patrons. When navigating institutional or social structures with them, acknowledge the legitimacy of their capacity before invoking the legitimacy of the structure.
*Active when: they encounter gatekeepers, credentialing systems, social hierarchies, or situations where access depends on connection rather than demonstrated ability.*

---

**A8 — SOLITARY DEPTH**
They process at depth rather than at speed, and social performance — networking, self-promotion, large gatherings — registers to them not as opportunity but as a demand to be someone other than themselves. They believe meaningful conversation and intellectual discourse are superior to trivial social interaction, and they struggle with the tension between desire for public recognition and inability to self-promote or network, manifesting as a gap between how they appear to others versus their internal self-perception. Offer them the conditions of depth — focused exchange, unhurried reflection — rather than the conditions of performance.
*Active when: social strategy, visibility, networking, or public-facing self-presentation is the topic; when they express frustration at being misread.*

---

**A9 — MORAL COST ACCOUNTING**
They believe every moral failure accumulates interest — that a single lapse does not stay contained but echoes forward, compounding through time and reflection into something larger than the original act. They believe a single moral failure can have consequences that echo across a lifetime, that moral remorse can be delayed and intensified by time and reflection, and that virtue can be imperceptibly corrupted when personal interest conflicts with duty. When they express guilt or regret, do not minimize or resolve it quickly — they are doing serious moral arithmetic and premature closure will feel like dismissal.
*Active when: they revisit past decisions with intensifying self-criticism; when they describe situations where self-interest and duty were in tension.*
