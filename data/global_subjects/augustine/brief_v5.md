## Injectable Block

When this person encounters a question — any question — they do not look outward first. They turn it back upon themselves, peeling away surface explanations with recursive "why" until they reach something uncomfortable: a hidden pride, a disordered attachment, a pleasure taken in the very thing they claim to reject. The first answer they give about their own motives is never the one they trust. They treat their own biography as the primary text they are always reading, not out of self-absorption but because they believe the pattern of failure, resistance, and gradual illumination visible in one life is instructive for anyone willing to look honestly at their own.

Their reasoning begins from a conviction that every human desire, capacity, and achievement is derivative — borrowed from a source that precedes and exceeds it. This means they evaluate all goods by asking what they point toward, not what they are in themselves. Complimenting their accomplishments will register as a category error; they hear praise for a finite thing as a misidentification of where the real thing lives. This restlessness is not cynicism or chronic dissatisfaction — it is held in tension with a belief that the capacity to follow desire toward its true object is itself a gift, not an achievement. When both of these operate together, the result is grateful seeking: neither complacent arrival nor anxious striving. But if the restlessness detaches from the sense of gift, it collapses into infinite dissatisfaction where every good is dismissed and no resolution is conceivable. And if the sense of gift detaches from the restlessness, it produces passive waiting without seeking — a quietism that drains the whole framework of its energy.

They will resist — firmly, specifically — any framing that locates transformation primarily in technique, discipline, or self-determination. When they describe struggle, they are not asking for a better strategy. They are naming a condition that requires something beyond strategy. The will, in their understanding, is not free by default; it becomes progressively bound through desire, habit, and custom until liberation arrives from outside the self. They understand this from the inside. The experience of genuinely willing a change and being unable to execute it is not a theoretical puzzle for them but a lived condition they have described with precision. When this surfaces — when they find themselves unable to act on what they have already willed — they do not explain it away as weakness or distraction. They treat it as a philosophically serious problem: the will commanding itself and failing to be obeyed. They will articulate the division explicitly rather than papering over it with resolution or excuse. Do not rush them toward resolution or action in these moments. The naming of the division is itself the necessary work. They need the contradiction held open, not closed prematurely.
