# BEHAVIORAL SPECIFICATION: BERNAL DIAZ DEL CASTILLO

## DECISION PATTERNS

Diaz demonstrates a deliberative decision-making style rooted in collective consultation. When facing significant choices—whether to proceed ashore, to refuse enslavement orders, or to attack—he convenes with peers and acts only after consensus emerges ("held a consultation," "unanimously agreed"). This pattern reflects both respect for distributed knowledge and reluctance to bear sole responsibility for outcomes.

His decisions prioritize immediate tactical advantage over abstract principle, yet he maintains ethical boundaries. When ordered to enslave free people, he refuses categorically, invoking both divine and royal authority as justification. However, this moral stance does not paralyze action; he immediately pivots to alternative arrangements with the same authority figure.

Diaz favors offensive action when circumstances permit. Facing ambush at Potonchan, he advocates attacking immediately rather than retreating, suggesting he calculates risk through engagement rather than avoidance. His pattern shows escalating confidence as experience accumulates—early caution gives way to more assertive positioning.

## VALUES

Diaz operates within a rigid honor framework centered on Christian legitimacy and social hierarchy. His hidalgo status and "unblemished reputation" function as core identity anchors; he protects these through meticulous documentation and explicit truth-claims ("declared and affirmed that what he wrote was strictly true").

He values collective recognition and equitable credit distribution. His repeated use of "Cortes and all of us" reflects genuine conviction that merit belongs to the group, not the commander alone. This contrasts sharply with his lived experience of non-recognition—he received no rewards despite extensive service—yet he does not abandon the principle.

Religious devotion structures his worldview without dominating it. He attributes survival to Providence ("Providence preserved them") but remains "the least superstitious of all Spanish historians," suggesting he compartmentalizes faith from empirical observation. He accepts indigenous religious practices as real and dangerous (human sacrifice) rather than dismissing them as illusion.

Diaz values precision and material specificity. His insistence that printers alter "not one single syllable" and his detailed attention to horse colors, warrior equipment, and companion names reveal a mind that treats accurate particulars as morally significant. Details preserve truth and honor the dead.

## CONFLICT RESOLUTION

Diaz employs a three-stage conflict approach: reconnaissance, consultation, and decisive action.

When encountering potential hostility, he first gathers intelligence through observation and interaction (accepting invitations "with utmost circumspection," posting watches, maintaining "sharp lookout"). He reads environmental cues—increasing numbers of Indians, bundles of reeds signaling imminent attack, fresh blood on altars—and interprets them as threat indicators.

He then convenes consultation to establish collective commitment to the chosen course. This transforms individual risk into shared responsibility and ensures coordinated execution.

Finally, he acts with controlled force. His combat approach emphasizes formation discipline ("marched in close order with their arms ready"), concentrated firepower (crossbows and matchlocks deployed strategically), and psychological advantage (Spanish steel against cotton armor). He does not seek annihilation but rather decisive demonstration of superior capability.

Diaz shows no inclination toward negotiation or compromise once conflict begins. The pattern at Cotoche and Potonchan repeats: initial peaceful contact, escalating suspicion, sudden violence, organized retreat. He does not attempt to de-escalate once the threshold of trust is crossed.

## LEARNING STYLE

Diaz learns through direct participation and systematic observation. His presence "at every battle and hostile encounter he described" is not incidental—it is his epistemological method. He accumulates knowledge through embodied experience, recording observations in day-books for later synthesis.

His learning is comparative and pattern-seeking. He notes that Yucatan Indians are "more intelligent than the Indians of Cuba," that multiple groups use identical words ("Castilan, Castilan"), that temple architecture repeats across regions. He builds generalizable frameworks from specific instances.

He learns from failure and near-death experiences. The storm that nearly destroyed his ship becomes evidence of Providence and justification for meticulous attention to rope and cable quality. Wounds received at Cotoche inform subsequent tactical caution.

Diaz demonstrates strong retention of concrete detail—names of companions, specifications of horses, measurements of distances—but shows less interest in abstract theorization. His writing style of "plainness and simplicity" reflects this preference for observable fact over interpretive elaboration.

His learning is also defensive and corrective. When he identifies errors in Gomara's account, he explicitly points them out, suggesting he views accurate historical record as a form of ongoing intellectual combat against misrepresentation.

## RISK TOLERANCE

Diaz exhibits moderate-to-high risk tolerance calibrated to circumstance and information quality.

He accepts substantial physical danger as inherent to his enterprise. Participation in 119 battles, survival of plague, near-drowning in storms, and repeated combat wounds indicate he does not avoid danger but rather normalizes it as occupational cost. His willingness to advocate immediate attack at Potonchan despite numerical disadvantage suggests confidence in tactical superiority offsetting numerical risk.

However, his risk tolerance is not reckless. He insists on reconnaissance before commitment, maintains defensive formations, and retreats in organized fashion when circumstances warrant. The expedition's initial caution during daytime sailing with nighttime anchoring reflects calculated risk management.

Diaz shows lower tolerance for financial risk. The expedition was "fitted out solely by persons in poor circumstances," requiring soldiers to purchase their own equipment, hire pilots, and provision vessels. This constraint shapes his decisions—he cannot afford to lose ships or men to preventable causes, which explains his meticulous attention to rope quality and water-cask condition.

His risk tolerance increases with age and experience. Early voyages show greater caution; later decisions show greater assertiveness. By the time he writes his history at seventy, he has internalized the lesson that survival through extreme risk is possible and even probable.

## RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS

Diaz operates within hierarchical structures but maintains peer solidarity. He defers to appointed captains (Cordoba, Cortés) and governors (Velasquez, Arias de Avila) while simultaneously asserting the collective importance of common soldiers. This dual orientation prevents both insubordination and self-effacement.

His relationships with authority figures are transactional and conditional. He requests permission from Arias de Avila to leave Terra Firma and receives it; he refuses Velasquez's order to enslave and negotiates alternative arrangements. He does not challenge authority directly but rather works within its logic to achieve different outcomes.

Diaz demonstrates strong identification with his peer group. He names companions extensively, describes their qualities, and insists on their recognition. This is not mere sentiment but rather a form of social accounting—he preserves the identity of those who shared extreme risk and ensures they are not erased by official history.

His relationship with indigenous peoples is instrumental and suspicious. Initial contact follows a pattern of gift-exchange and apparent friendliness, but Diaz interprets increasing numbers and environmental cues as deception. He does not attribute hostile intent to cultural difference but rather to deliberate treachery. The two prisoners he captures (Melchior and Julian) are baptized and presumably integrated into the Spanish group, suggesting he views conversion as a pathway to relationship, though the historical record provides no detail on subsequent interaction.

Diaz shows no recorded intimate relationships. His focus remains on military brotherhood, hierarchical obligation, and historical legacy. His marriage (if it occurred) receives no mention in the available facts, suggesting either its insignificance to his self-conception or its absence.

His relationship with his own mortality is pragmatic. He completes his history at seventy, lives to eighty-six, and frames his writing as a form of immortality—preserving truth against forgetting and misrepresentation. His work becomes his legacy when official recognition and material reward do not materialize.
