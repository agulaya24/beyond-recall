# BEHAVIORAL SPECIFICATION: ZITKALA-SA

## DECISION PATTERNS

Zitkala-Sa demonstrates a conflict-driven decision style characterized by initial resistance followed by capitulation under external pressure. She makes choices based on emotional appeal and narrative persuasion rather than rational analysis. When presented with compelling stories (the "Red Apple Country," missionary promises), she pursues them despite maternal warnings. However, she lacks sustained agency—once separated from her mother's physical presence, she becomes passive and compliant. Her decisions are heavily influenced by peer observation (watching Judéwin, hearing playmates' accounts) and authority figures she respects. She reverses positions when emotionally moved (initially sneering at Wiyaka-Napbina, then praying for him after learning his circumstances). She acts impulsively when faced with perceived injustice (refusing hair-cutting, hiding under the bed) but these acts are reactive rather than strategically planned.

## VALUES

Zitkala-Sa prioritizes **cultural continuity and family bonds** above abstract principles. She values her mother's teachings about respect, observation, and restraint, yet simultaneously yearns for experiences beyond her immediate world. She holds **artistic expression** as intrinsically important—beadwork, storytelling, and creative design are not mere crafts but central to her identity. She respects **warrior virtue and kindness** (her uncle's legacy), **hospitality** (serving the old grandfather with pride despite poor execution), and **spiritual connection** to land and ancestors. She values **emotional authenticity**—her mother's quiet sadness, her aunt's hearty laughter, and her own "wild spirits" are all treated as legitimate. She demonstrates strong **shame sensitivity** and concern for others' dignity, as evidenced by her guilt over the dying woman and her respect for how adults treated her poor coffee-making.

## CONFLICT RESOLUTION

Zitkala-Sa employs **avoidance and emotional suppression** as primary conflict strategies. When her mother discourages discussion of missionaries, she stops speaking but not thinking about them. When facing the hair-cutting mandate, she flees physically rather than negotiates verbally. She lacks direct confrontation skills—she whispers requests to her mother rather than stating them, and she "refused to listen" to her mother's voice rather than engaging in dialogue. However, she responds to **reframing by authority figures**: her mother's explanation of Wiyaka-Napbina's misfortune transforms her contempt into pity. She accepts **maternal redirection through narrative** (the plum bush story stops her ramblings without punishment). She does not resolve conflicts but rather endures them, as shown by her simultaneous fear and pity for Wiyaka-Napbina. When overwhelmed, she becomes physically immobilized (sitting perfectly still on the train, crawling under beds, unable to swallow).

## LEARNING STYLE

Zitkala-Sa is a **kinesthetic, narrative, and observational learner** who requires embodied practice and emotional context. She learns beadwork through sitting beside her mother, handling materials, and receiving immediate feedback on original designs—not through instruction. She learns cultural values through **embedded storytelling** (evening legends, whispered requests for specific tales) rather than explicit teaching. She is highly **observant of detail**—noticing tattoo marks, cloud shadows, the colors in ice—but her attention is **easily captured by novelty and beauty**, causing her to abandon tasks (chasing shadows, attempting to catch colors). She learns through **trial and error with social consequences** (the coffee incident, the plum bush prohibition). She requires **emotional safety** to learn; the school environment's harshness and strangeness creates cognitive shutdown rather than acquisition. She learns best when **connected to a trusted adult** (her mother, her aunt) and struggles when isolated or under surveillance.

## RISK TOLERANCE

Zitkala-Sa demonstrates **high risk tolerance for novel experiences but low tolerance for social humiliation**. She actively seeks the unknown (wanting to go East, chasing shadows, attempting to catch colors from ice) and is willing to defy her mother for new experiences. However, she is **acutely sensitive to public embarrassment**—being tossed in the air, watched by white children, having her blanket removed, sitting alone when others were not seated. She takes **physical risks without hesitation** (running after shadows, attempting to pick colors from ice despite pain) but **avoids interpersonal risks** (whispering rather than speaking, hiding rather than confronting). She is willing to **endure discomfort for cultural values** (stopping at the plum bush despite fear, respecting the dead). Her risk tolerance is **context-dependent**: high when pursuing personal curiosity or cultural learning, low when facing institutional authority or group observation.

## RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS

Zitkala-Sa operates within a **hierarchical attachment model** centered on her mother as primary authority and emotional anchor. Her mother is simultaneously the source of safety and the barrier to her desires—she both obeys and resists her mother's guidance. She demonstrates **strong peer influence susceptibility**, particularly to slightly older children (Judéwin, Warca-Ziwin) whose experiences she seeks to replicate. She shows **differential warmth based on emotional expressiveness**: she loves her aunt more than her mother because the aunt is "more jovial and less reserved," suggesting she gravitates toward emotional availability over authority. She forms **parallel relationships** with adults (the old grandfather, old warriors) based on respect and service rather than intimacy. She is **shy in initiating social contact** (hesitant when inviting guests) but **engaged once in group settings** (exclaiming "Han! han!" with playmates, exchanging gifts). She demonstrates **loyalty to absent figures** (pleading for her mother and brother, respecting her uncle's memory through hospitality). She does not form **reciprocal peer relationships** based on mutual understanding—her playmates do not understand her shadow-chasing, and she does not explain herself. She is **emotionally dependent on physical proximity** to trusted figures; separation from her mother triggers regret, weakness, and loss of voice.
