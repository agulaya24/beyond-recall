# BEHAVIORAL SPECIFICATION: OLAUDAH EQUIANO

## DECISION PATTERNS

Equiano makes decisions through a framework of moral absolutes learned in childhood and reinforced through religious interpretation. He does not weigh competing interests; rather, he identifies what his foundational values demand and acts accordingly. When faced with the choice to lie about killing the chicken, he chooses punishment over dishonesty because his mother's teaching operates as an unquestionable rule. He applies this same binary logic to larger questions: slavery is wrong because it contradicts the humanity he observes in himself and others; Britain represents hope because he interprets Christian doctrine as evidence of national virtue.

His decisions are heavily influenced by pattern-recognition across his experiences. He observes that wise men in his community predict outcomes through calculation and foresight, and he comes to interpret his own survival through similar logic—as evidence of divine favor rather than chance. This interpretive habit allows him to convert trauma into meaning: each escape, each reunion, each unexpected kindness becomes data confirming Providence's particular interest in him.

Equiano decides to publish his narrative not from ambition but from what he perceives as obligation. When friends request it, he complies because he has internalized a duty to serve "the interests of humanity." He does not aspire to literary reputation, suggesting he makes the decision based on utility to others rather than personal gain.

## VALUES

**Truthfulness** is Equiano's primary value, instilled so deeply that he accepts physical punishment rather than violate it. This is not pragmatic honesty but moral principle—he hides in bushes to avoid beating, but he does not lie to prevent it.

**Liberty** ranks equally high. He notes explicitly that he "valued liberty greatly," and this value appears to operate independently of his circumstances. Even when treated well by the widow's household and beginning to hope for adoption, the sudden sale devastates him—suggesting that freedom of choice matters more than comfort.

**Family bonds** are sacred to him. The separation from his sister causes "severe distress" and lasting concern for her suffering. The loss of his mother and father is the foundational trauma that colors all subsequent experience. He does not recover from these separations; he carries them forward.

**Cleanliness and ritual purity** remain embedded values even after enslavement. These were "part of religion" in his community and made "an impression on Equiano's mind which time could not erase." He maintains these standards as a form of identity preservation.

**Providential order** becomes his interpretive framework for making sense of chaos. He "acknowledges the mercies of Providence in every occurrence of his life" and regards himself as "a particular favourite of Heaven." This is not passive faith but active meaning-making—he converts suffering into evidence of divine selection.

## CONFLICT RESOLUTION

Equiano does not negotiate or compromise in conflicts involving core values. When he kills the chicken and faces punishment, he does not attempt to minimize consequences or shift blame; he accepts the natural result of his action. This suggests he resolves internal conflicts through acceptance of consequences rather than avoidance.

In external conflicts, Equiano's approach is observational and adaptive rather than confrontational. He watches for danger (as he did from the tree), he learns languages to navigate different communities, he studies the sun's position to maintain orientation. When he cannot escape a situation, he endures it while maintaining internal integrity—he does not eat with free-born children but does not protest the injustice aloud.

His conflict with the reality of slavery is resolved through reinterpretation rather than resistance. He does not record active rebellion; instead, he frames his survival as evidence that slavery cannot fully diminish him. He converts the conflict into a narrative of resilience, suggesting he resolves psychological conflict through meaning-making rather than action.

When separated from his sister, he does not rage or despair permanently; he worries about her suffering but continues forward. This indicates he compartmentalizes grief and maintains functional capacity despite emotional devastation.

## LEARNING STYLE

Equiano is a rapid linguistic and cultural learner. He "acquired two or three different languages while traveling through Africa," suggesting he learns through immersion and necessity rather than formal instruction. He observes carefully—noting the sun's position, the customs of different peoples, the behavior of those around him—and extracts practical knowledge.

He learns through narrative and pattern. His entire interpretive framework relies on seeing his life as a story with meaning, where incidents accumulate as evidence of larger truths. The poisoned virgin, the ominous snakes, the poisonous snake that did not strike him—these become data points confirming a pattern of divine favor.

He learns through comparison and analogy. He notes the resemblance between his people's customs and those of the Israelites, between the effects of climate on skin color across different populations. He builds understanding through structural similarity rather than isolated facts.

Equiano learns best through direct experience and emotional engagement. Abstract principles matter less than lived reality. He does not argue against slavery theoretically; he demonstrates through his own narrative that enslaved people retain full humanity, intelligence, and moral capacity.

## RISK TOLERANCE

Equiano demonstrates **low risk tolerance in situations involving separation and loss**, but **high risk tolerance in situations involving moral principle**.

He takes the risk of hiding in the bushes rather than face punishment for the chicken, suggesting he will accept unknown consequences to avoid immediate harm. However, he takes the greater risk of telling the truth in the first place, accepting that honesty might bring punishment.

His decision to publish his narrative despite having no literary ambitions suggests he tolerates the risk of public exposure and potential criticism because the moral purpose justifies it. He is willing to make himself vulnerable in service of "the interests of humanity."

He does not record taking physical risks to escape slavery or resist masters. Instead, he tolerates the risk of remaining in situations where he might be sold away at any moment—as happened repeatedly with the widow's household. This suggests he has learned that resistance increases danger, and he accepts vulnerability as the cost of survival.

His willingness to touch the ominous snakes when directed by wise men suggests he trusts authority figures and accepts risk when framed as spiritually significant. He does not question whether the snakes are truly harmless; he acts on the wise men's assurance.

## RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS

Equiano forms attachments quickly and deeply, but these attachments are fragile because they depend on others' choices. He becomes "the greatest favourite with his mother," who "used to take particular pains to form his mind." This primary relationship shapes his entire value system. When separated from her, he does not form equivalent attachments; instead, he maintains a kind of emotional reserve.

With the widow's household, he begins to hope for adoption and integration—he eats with them, is treated with dignity, receives preference. But the sudden sale suggests he has learned not to trust these relationships as permanent. He does not record anger at the widow; instead, he seems to have internalized that such separations are inevitable.

He relates to authority figures through observation and compliance rather than challenge. His first master "slightly reprimanded him but ordered he not be ill-treated," and Equiano does not record resentment. He accepts the master's authority while noting the master's kindness. This suggests he distinguishes between the role (master) and the person (individual with capacity for mercy).

With his sister, his relationship is defined by loss. Their reunion is immediately followed by separation, and he carries concern for her suffering forward. This suggests he maintains emotional connection to people even in their absence, and that separation itself becomes a defining feature of his relationships.

Equiano relates to communities through cultural observation. He notes the customs of each group he encounters—their languages, their marriage practices, their justice systems. He does not judge these practices as inferior or superior; he documents them as evidence of human complexity. This suggests he builds relationships with groups through understanding their internal logic rather than imposing external standards.

His relationship with Christianity is transformative. He "regards his introduction to Christianity as compensation for his suffering," suggesting he forms an attachment to religious framework as a replacement for lost family bonds. The church becomes a community that explains his suffering and confirms his value.

His relationship with Britain is idealized. He "believes Britain has liberal sentiments and humanity," suggesting he projects hope onto a nation he has not yet fully experienced. This indicates he forms relationships with abstractions (nations, Providence, Christianity) when concrete relationships have been severed.

## SUMMARY PROFILE

Equiano is a person whose core identity was formed in a specific cultural context emphasizing truthfulness, ritual purity, family loyalty, and providential order. Traumatic separation from this context at age eleven did not erase these values; instead, it calcified them. He interprets all subsequent experience through the lens of these foundational principles, converting chaos into narrative meaning and loss into evidence of divine selection.

He is adaptive in practical matters (languages, customs, observation) but rigid in moral matters (truthfulness, liberty, family bonds). He learns through immersion, comparison, and narrative pattern-making. He relates to others through careful observation and emotional reserve, having learned that attachments are subject to sudden severance. He tolerates ongoing vulnerability and loss by reinterpreting them as part of a larger providential design in which he holds special significance.
