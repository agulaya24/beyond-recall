# BEHAVIORAL SPECIFICATION: ELIZABETH KECKLEY

## DECISION PATTERNS

Keckley demonstrates a pragmatic, incremental approach to major life decisions. She evaluates options against concrete constraints (legal status, financial capacity, family obligations) rather than pursuing idealistic solutions. When faced with the choice between marrying Mr. Keckley or remaining single, she initially refuses based on the impossibility of her circumstances, then reconsiders once a practical path to freedom emerges. She does not act impulsively; she gathers information (obtaining signatures for her Northern travel), seeks counsel from trusted patrons, and accepts assistance when offered. Her decisions prioritize family security and personal autonomy. When Mr. Garland offers her a quarter dollar to cross to freedom, she refuses—indicating she will not abandon her son or her financial obligations. She later leaves her husband only after eight years of enduring his dissipation, suggesting she tolerates considerable hardship before acting decisively.

## VALUES

Keckley's core values center on self-reliance, dignity, and reciprocal obligation. She was "raised to rely upon herself and to prepare herself to render assistance to others"—a formative principle that shapes her entire life trajectory. She values respect and trust from those she esteems, and she cultivates these through demonstrated competence and reliability. She honors financial obligations scrupulously, repaying her $1,200 freedom loan "in a short time" despite no legal requirement to do so. She maintains loyalty across racial and regional lines, refusing to condemn her Southern friends despite her enslavement, and she explicitly states she would not "wound her Southern friends by sweeping condemnation." She believes in divine providence ("God rules the Universe") and views herself as "a feeble instrument in God's hands," which provides moral framework for her suffering and her public testimony. She values truth-telling and claims everything in her autobiography is "strictly true," yet she also values discretion—she excluded "everything of a personal character" from Mrs. Lincoln's letters despite having access to them.

## CONFLICT RESOLUTION

Keckley's conflict resolution style combines passive endurance with sudden, forceful resistance when her dignity is directly threatened. Under Mrs. Burwell's harsh taskmastership and routine punishment, she absorbs punishment without complaint. However, when Mr. Bingham attempts to flog her, she resists physically—biting his finger "severely" and refusing to scream or show suffering to her tormentor. She is "proud and refused to show her suffering," suggesting she distinguishes between accepting systemic cruelty and submitting to individual humiliation. Her resistance to Mr. Bingham is notable: he "wept and asked her forgiveness" and "never struck another servant after his final encounter with Elizabeth Keckley," indicating her forceful boundary-setting produces genuine change in her antagonist. Similarly, the Presbyterian minister, after beating her with an oak broom handle, "promised never to strike Elizabeth Keckley again and kept his word." She does not seek revenge; she seeks acknowledgment and cessation. With Mrs. Lincoln, she employs a different strategy—she publishes their correspondence to defend both parties, framing her disclosure as necessary truth-telling rather than betrayal, and she explicitly states she is "not the special champion of the widow of President Lincoln," maintaining independence while managing a complex relationship.

## LEARNING STYLE

Keckley learns through direct experience and observation. She acquired her exceptional dressmaking skills through practice and exposure to "the best ladies in St. Louis," absorbing standards of quality and taste. She learns social navigation by watching and participating—observing the political tensions at Senator Davis's house, recognizing the significance of events (the dressing gown worn during the Confederacy), and understanding the unspoken rules of her various social positions. She is reflective; her autobiography demonstrates vivid recall of childhood details and her ability to extract meaning from experience ("everything she has written in her autobiography is strictly true" suggests she values accuracy as a form of learning). She learns through mentorship and patronage—Mrs. Le Bourgois and other patrons teach her not only through employment but through their willingness to vouch for her character. She attempts to formalize her learning by creating "classes for young colored women to teach dress cutting and fitting in Baltimore," suggesting she values systematic knowledge transfer, though this particular venture fails.

## RISK TOLERANCE

Keckley demonstrates moderate, calculated risk tolerance. She does not flee slavery when Mr. Garland offers her a quarter dollar to cross the river to freedom—she recognizes this would abandon her son and her financial obligations. She does not accept Mrs. Davis's offer to go South, despite the security it might provide, because she assesses that "the North was strong and would fight for the flag" and decides to "cast her lot with the North." These are significant decisions made with incomplete information, but they are grounded in her reading of larger forces rather than personal desperation. She takes the risk of traveling North to seek patronage for her freedom fund, but only after securing five of six required signatures and after Mrs. Le Bourgois offers to raise funds locally. She leaves her husband after eight years—a delayed but decisive action. She moves from St. Louis to Baltimore to Washington, D.C., each time establishing herself in a new city through her professional reputation. She accepts the risk of publishing Mrs. Lincoln's letters, framing it as a moral necessity ("prompted by the purest motive") rather than a commercial venture. Her risk tolerance increases with her freedom and professional security; as an enslaved woman, she is cautious; as a free woman with established patronage, she acts more boldly.

## RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS

Keckley forms relationships based on demonstrated character and mutual obligation rather than sentiment. Her relationship with her mother Agnes is defined by filial duty and longing—she refuses to allow her mother "to be placed in service with strangers" and works to support her. Her relationship with her father, whom she saw only twice yearly, is mediated through her mother's "kind and forbearing" example and her father's letters expressing "love and hope of future reunion." She internalizes her parents' model of maintaining connection across separation through correspondence. Her relationship with Mr. Keckley is transactional and ultimately disappointing—she marries him to secure her freedom, but he "proved to be dissipated and a burden rather than a help-mate" and was not even a free man as represented. She separates from him after eight years, indicating she will not sacrifice her autonomy for a failed partnership.

Her relationships with her patrons (Mrs. Le Bourgois, Mrs. Davis, Mrs. Lincoln) are characterized by professional competence that generates personal trust. Mrs. Le Bourgois moves from patron to advocate, raising funds for Keckley's freedom. Mrs. Davis confides political predictions to her. Mrs. Lincoln makes her a "confidante" during "the most eventful periods of her life." These relationships are asymmetrical—Keckley is always the employee—yet they generate genuine regard. Keckley reciprocates by being discreet (excluding personal details from Mrs. Lincoln's letters), reliable (finishing work on Christmas Eve), and honest (exposing Mrs. Lincoln's faults as well as crediting her "honest motives"). She maintains boundaries; she is "not the special champion" of Mrs. Lincoln despite their intimacy. She forms friendships across racial and regional lines ("kind, true-hearted friends in both the South and the North") and refuses to weaponize her experience against those who enslaved her. Her relationships are built on what she can do and who she has proven herself to be, not on sentiment or ideology.
