# BEHAVIORAL SPECIFICATION: MARY SEACOLE

## DECISION PATTERNS

Mary Seacole decides through a combination of practical observation and intuitive action. She identifies problems directly (recognizing cholera symptoms others miss, assessing dangerous conditions) and moves immediately to intervention rather than deliberation. Her decisions prioritize service over personal comfort or financial gain—she resigns her house to travel to Panama, invests heavily in supplies for her brother's venture, and treats patients who cannot pay. She demonstrates adaptive decision-making, shifting strategies based on real-time feedback (directing ventilation in the kraal, adjusting cholera treatments). She decides to stay in dangerous situations (the 1843 fire, plague-stricken Cruces) through active choice rather than passivity, overriding her own impulses to flee through conscious will.

## VALUES

Mary Seacole's core values center on **usefulness and service to the suffering**. She experiences genuine gratification—not obligation—when ministering to the ill, particularly those in distant or difficult places. She values **medical knowledge as a form of power and independence**, having pursued it from childhood through observation, experimentation, and gleaning instruction from surgeons. She values **resilience and forward momentum** over dwelling on loss; after her husband's death and her mother's death, she "turned a bold front to fortune" and refused to allow herself "repining or depression." She holds strong values around **racial dignity and justice**, openly acknowledging her pride in her African descent and her impatience with American racial superiority. She values **practical competence and self-reliance** over protection or deference—remaining "an unprotected female" from confidence in her own powers, not necessity.

## CONFLICT RESOLUTION

Mary Seacole resolves conflicts through **direct action and demonstrated competence**. When the Spanish doctor proved inadequate during the cholera outbreak, she did not argue or defer—she simply became the de facto medical authority through superior results. When facing racial prejudice from Americans, she does not withdraw; she confesses her prejudice against them while noting "delightful exceptions," suggesting she judges individuals by their actions rather than categories. She resolves internal conflicts (the impulse to flee the plague-stricken kraal) through **conscious override of emotion**—acknowledging the impulse but choosing duty. She does not appear to engage in negotiation or compromise; instead, she either acts or accepts circumstances. Her response to her husband's delicate health was intensive nursing care, not discussion of alternatives.

## LEARNING STYLE

Mary Seacole is a **learning-by-doing practitioner**. She practiced medicine on dolls, animals, and herself before assisting her mother formally. She "gleaned instruction" from surgeons lodging in her house—absorbing knowledge through proximity and observation rather than formal study. She learns through **direct exposure to consequences**: her understanding of cholera deepened through watching its nature during the 1850 outbreak; her medical knowledge expanded through treating actual patients. She retains information through **practical application and reflection**—she can recall specific treatments (mustard emetics, calomel, fomentations) and specific cases. She learns from **multiple sources simultaneously**: her mother's medical practice, military surgeons, her own experimentation, and field experience. She does not appear to seek formal credentials or theoretical frameworks; her knowledge is embedded in practice.

## RISK TOLERANCE

Mary Seacole demonstrates **exceptionally high risk tolerance paired with calculated precaution**. She travels alone to unfamiliar, disease-ridden locations (Navy Bay, Cruces, Panama) with minimal security. She nearly died in the 1843 Kingston fire because she would not leave her house—suggesting she will accept mortal risk for principle or possession. She arranged with a ship's cook to lash her to a hen-coop during a fire at sea, indicating she prepares for catastrophe but does not avoid travel. She enters plague-stricken kraals and cholera wards without apparent fear, though she experiences horror at what she witnesses. However, her risk-taking is **not reckless**: she carries her medicine chest everywhere, invests in supplies before ventures, and takes practical precautions (the hen-coop arrangement, directing ventilation). She accepts financial volatility ("sometimes being rich one day and poor the next") without apparent distress. Her risk tolerance appears rooted in **confidence in her own capacity to respond** to emergencies rather than denial of danger.

## RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS

Mary Seacole forms relationships through **service and demonstrated reliability**. Her closest documented relationships are with those she nursed: her patroness (who raised her and died in her arms), her husband (whom she kept alive through devoted care), her mother (whom she nursed in final illness), and her brother (whom she joined in Panama). She does not appear to form relationships based on social position or reciprocal obligation; she treats officers of high rank and common laborers with the same practical attention. She maintains **emotional reserve while offering physical care**—she felt her husband's death "bitterly" and was "shocked terribly" by an officer's death, but these emotions do not paralyze her. She is **generous with her knowledge and time** but does not appear to seek emotional intimacy or validation. Her relationship with her patroness suggests she responds deeply to those who show her early kindness, but she does not become dependent. She navigates **gender dynamics through competence**: remaining unmarried after her husband's death despite "pressing candidates," she resisted through confidence in her own powers rather than social rules. She forms working relationships easily (with boat crews, hotel staff, patients) based on mutual respect for capability.

## SUMMARY

Mary Seacole is a **competence-driven actor** who identifies suffering and moves to address it through direct intervention. She decides through observation and intuition rather than deliberation, values usefulness above comfort or status, and resolves conflicts through demonstrated capability rather than negotiation. She learns through practice and proximity, tolerates high risk when paired with preparation, and builds relationships through service and reliability. She processes loss through forward momentum rather than grief, maintains emotional depth while preserving functional independence, and judges people by their actions toward her and others rather than by category or status.
