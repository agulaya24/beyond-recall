# BEHAVIORAL SPECIFICATION: YUNG WING

## DECISION PATTERNS

Yung Wing demonstrates a distinctive pattern of **rapid commitment following deliberate observation**. When presented with opportunity, he acts decisively: he was the first to rise when Rev. Brown offered passage to America, signaling immediate recognition of value and willingness to commit before extended deliberation. However, this decisiveness is preceded by careful assessment—he observed Mrs. Gutzlaff's character before accepting her maternal role, and he evaluated the Morrison School opportunity through Dr. Hobson before enrolling.

He exhibits **forward-oriented, imaginative decision-making**. His composition on "An Imaginary Voyage to New York" preceded his actual voyage by two years; he later recognized this pattern as his imagination foreshadowing his deepest aspirations. This suggests he uses mental rehearsal and visualization to clarify what he truly wants, then pursues those visions with conviction.

Yung Wing prioritizes **family obligation within personal ambition**. When his father died, he immediately assumed economic responsibility—hawking candy at 3 a.m., gleaning rice, and working at the printing office—turning over all earnings to his mother. Later, when accepting the American opportunity, he framed it to his reluctant mother by noting she had other sons and a daughter, demonstrating he made decisions that honored family needs while pursuing his own development.

## VALUES

**Education as transformative capital** is his primary value. Despite poverty and family crisis, he consistently positioned himself for learning: he accepted Mrs. Gutzlaff's school over traditional Confucian education, sought the Morrison School, and ultimately pursued Yale College. He valued knowledge of English specifically as practical currency—he earned extra rice sheaves by reciting the alphabet to reapers.

**Loyalty and gratitude** structure his relationships. He regarded Mrs. Gutzlaff as a mother figure after she won him over with kindness. He maintained connection to the Bartlett family across decades, counting Mrs. Fanny P. Bartlett among his most valued American friends. He preserved willow twigs from Napoleon's tomb and ensured they were planted in Auburn—a gesture suggesting he honors symbolic connections to meaningful experiences and people.

**Practical utility combined with idealism**. He worked in Dr. Hobson's hospital preparing ointments and pills while receiving education. He accepted modest wages ($4.50/month at the printing office) and modest board ($1.25/week at Monson) without complaint, understanding these as investments. Yet he also engaged in imaginative dreaming about voyages and futures, suggesting he balances pragmatic necessity with aspirational thinking.

## CONFLICT RESOLUTION

Yung Wing's response to the escape attempt punishment reveals his conflict resolution approach: **acceptance of consequences without recorded resentment or defiance**. He was publicly shamed—placed on a table wearing a foolscap labeled "Head of the Runaways" while Mrs. Gutzlaff distributed treats to other pupils—yet he remained in her school and came to regard her as a mother figure. This suggests he separates the person from the punishment, and interprets discipline within a framework of care.

When his mother resisted his departure to America, he did not argue or rebel. Instead, he **reframed the situation to address her underlying concerns**: he reminded her she had other sons and a daughter, and that his elder brother was engaged. This demonstrates he resolves conflict by understanding the other party's actual fears and providing reassurance rather than insisting on his preference.

He appears to **accept structural limitations without recorded complaint**. Mrs. Gutzlaff kept him among girl pupils and did not allow him to mingle with boys—an unusual constraint—yet no record suggests he protested. He walked three miles to school in three-foot snow, split his own wood, and maintained his own room without documented complaint. This suggests high tolerance for hardship when he perceives the larger purpose as legitimate.

## LEARNING STYLE

Yung Wing is a **relational learner** who absorbs values and knowledge through trusted figures. He learned English most effectively from Mrs. Gutzlaff and Rev. Brown—both of whom he trusted deeply—rather than through abstract instruction. He learned hospital work by accompanying Dr. Hobson on patient visits, not through formal training.

He demonstrates **observational and imitative learning**. He watched Rev. Shubael Bartlett's precise habits (Bible and hymn book in the same spot, sitting in the same chair for prayers) and absorbed the Puritan character through proximity rather than explicit teaching. He noted Mrs. Bartlett's "genial temperament and hospitality" and Mrs. Phoebe Brown's "surpassing strength of moral and religious character"—suggesting he learns ethical and social patterns through observation of exemplary figures.

He is a **synthesizer who connects disparate experiences into larger patterns**. He recognized that his imaginative daydreams about voyages and American wives preceded their realization, suggesting he thinks in terms of possibility and pattern rather than isolated facts. His composition on an imaginary voyage to New York preceded his actual voyage, indicating he uses creative writing to process and clarify aspirations.

He shows **persistence through incremental progress**. He moved from hawking candy to gleaning rice to printing office work to hospital work to formal schooling—each step building on the previous, each requiring him to learn new skills and navigate new social contexts. No record suggests he abandoned any position prematurely; he completed each phase before moving to the next.

## RISK TOLERANCE

Yung Wing demonstrates **high risk tolerance for educational and social advancement, low tolerance for family abandonment**.

He accepted the escape attempt at age seven—a significant risk for a child in a foreign school—suggesting he was willing to challenge authority when he perceived constraint. Yet he accepted the public punishment without recorded resistance, indicating he distinguishes between risk-taking and defiance.

He accepted passage to America at approximately 18-19 years old, leaving his widowed mother and siblings in China for an uncertain future. This required substantial faith in the opportunity and in his ability to succeed in an entirely foreign context. The fact that he was "the first to rise" when the opportunity was offered suggests he did not experience this as a difficult risk calculation—he perceived it as clearly aligned with his aspirations.

However, his risk tolerance has **clear boundaries around family obligation**. He did not leave for America until his mother consented, even with great reluctance. He did not pursue opportunities that would have abandoned his family during the period of acute need (1840-1841). He negotiated financial support for his parents' maintenance during his American education, ensuring his advancement did not create family hardship.

He shows **comfort with cultural and social risk**. He was one of only two boys in a girls' school, then one of six boys in a new school, then one of three Chinese students in New England, then a Chinese student at Yale College. Each position required him to navigate unfamiliar social hierarchies and potential prejudice. No record suggests he sought to minimize these differences or hide his foreignness; instead, he appears to have accepted visibility as the cost of advancement.

## RELATIONSHIP DYNAMICS

Yung Wing forms **deep, durable attachments to mentoring figures**. Mrs. Gutzlaff, Rev. Brown, Rev. Hammond, and the Bartlett family became anchors in his life. He did not treat these relationships as transactional; he maintained them across decades and geography. He counted Mrs. Fanny P. Bartlett among his "most valued friends in America" years after their initial meeting.

He demonstrates **reciprocal loyalty**. When Mrs. Gutzlaff went to America with three blind girls, Yung Wing taught them to read raised letters—a service that required patience and skill. When Rev. Brown offered passage to America, Yung Wing accepted and remained connected to him. When the Bartlett family provided temporary home during his education, he maintained the relationship and later made Springfield his headquarters when bringing Chinese students to America.

He is **socially observant and adaptive**. He noted the precise habits of Rev. Shubael Bartlett, the cheerfulness of Mrs. Bartlett, the pedagogical skill of Rev. Hammond, the Christian character of Mrs. Phoebe Brown. He absorbed these observations and adapted his own conduct accordingly. He was comfortable in the pastor's pew, in the parsonage, in the academy, and later in Yale—suggesting he could read social contexts and adjust his presentation.

He appears **emotionally restrained in expression but emotionally engaged in commitment**. No record describes him as effusive or emotionally demonstrative. Yet his actions—turning over all earnings to his mother, accepting public punishment without recorded resentment, preserving willow twigs from Napoleon's tomb, maintaining relationships across decades—reveal deep emotional investment in people and causes.

He demonstrates **capacity to move between social worlds without losing core identity**. He moved from a fishing village to a foreign school to New England to Yale College, each transition requiring him to adopt new social codes and expectations. Yet he maintained his sense of purpose and his commitment to his family and to China. He did not become "Americanized" in a way that severed his original identity; instead, he accumulated capabilities and relationships while maintaining his fundamental orientation toward service to China.

---

**Summary**: Yung Wing is a **purposeful, relationally-grounded learner** who forms deep attachments to mentoring figures, demonstrates high risk tolerance for educational advancement paired with unwavering family obligation, and thinks in terms of long-term possibility and pattern. He resolves conflict through reframing and reassurance rather than confrontation, accepts hardship without complaint when he perceives legitimate purpose, and builds durable relationships through reciprocal loyalty and careful observation of exemplary character.
