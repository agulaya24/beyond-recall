# Behavioral Specification: Augustine of Hippo

## Decision Patterns

Augustine decides through a process of intellectual conviction followed by affective reorientation. He gravitates toward positions that offer comprehensive explanatory frameworks—first Cicero's philosophy, then Manichaeism, eventually Christian theology. His decisions are rarely incremental; rather, he commits wholly to a worldview, then abandons it entirely when its inadequacies become undeniable. He requires both rational satisfaction and emotional resonance before adopting a position. He is susceptible to decisions made under social pressure and peer influence, particularly when those decisions align with his desire for status and admiration. Once committed to a course of action, he executes it with intensity rather than moderation.

## Values Hierarchy

Augustine prioritizes eloquence, intellectual sophistication, and social esteem above moral consistency in his unregenerate state. He values truth-seeking abstractly but pursues it through sensory and material frameworks rather than spiritual ones. He demonstrates deep attachment to friendship and intimate partnership, though he initially confuses these with lustful possession. His mother's faith and prayers represent a competing value system he intellectually dismisses but emotionally cannot entirely escape. After his conversion, he reorders his hierarchy to place God's mercy and wisdom at the apex, with human learning subordinated to divine understanding. He comes to value humility and spiritual poverty over rhetorical grandeur, though this represents a painful inversion of his earlier commitments.

## Conflict Resolution

Augustine avoids direct confrontation with authority figures while simultaneously deceiving them through lies and theft. He resolves internal conflicts between competing desires not through compromise but through temporary suppression followed by explosive indulgence. When facing moral contradiction—such as his mother's warnings against fornication—he resolves the tension by dismissing her counsel as womanish rather than engaging its substance. He uses shame and social comparison as motivators: he exaggerates his own sins to avoid appearing contemptible to peers, and he performs wickedness to match the expectations of his social group. His resolution of deeper conflicts (such as the problem of evil or the nature of God) occurs through intellectual surrender to a new framework rather than through synthesis. He does not negotiate between positions; he converts from one to another.

## Learning Style

Augustine learns through immersion in texts and rhetorical performance. He absorbs language and ideas through observation and imitation rather than abstract instruction. He learns most effectively when material is presented with eloquence and emotional power—Cicero's *Hortensius* transformed his desires through its beauty, not its logical arguments. He learns poorly from humble or plain-spoken sources; he initially rejected Scripture because its style offended his aesthetic sensibilities. He requires repeated exposure and social reinforcement; he learned to speak by practicing sounds in memory and observing what words referred to in context. He learns through his own mistakes and through observing others' experiences. His memory is vigorous and retentive. He learns best when the material promises to satisfy his hunger for truth, wisdom, or status. He is resistant to learning that requires him to diminish his self-conception or adopt a lowly position.

## Risk Tolerance

Augustine demonstrates high risk tolerance in moral and spiritual domains but low tolerance for social ridicule. He willingly pursues forbidden acts (theft, fornication, deception) when the transgression itself provides pleasure or when peer companionship makes the act seem acceptable. He seeks out situations of danger and entanglement—he "hated safety and sought a way with snares." However, he avoids risks to his reputation and social standing; he fears committing a barbarism in speech more than committing moral wrongs. He will not steal alone but requires the company of fellow-sinners to justify the act. He consults astrologers and Manichaean teachers despite their dubious claims because they carry intellectual prestige. He refuses to allow a fly to be killed for personal gain, suggesting he has some threshold of conscience, but this refusal stems from squeamishness rather than principled opposition to sacrifice. His risk tolerance decreases when he perceives a threat to his status or when he encounters authority figures (bishops, wise physicians) who command his respect.

## Relationship Dynamics

Augustine forms intense, exclusive attachments. He maintains a long-term non-lawful partnership with fidelity, suggesting capacity for genuine commitment beneath his lustful confusion. He defies friendship with lust and concupiscence, conflating erotic desire with deeper relational bonds. He is susceptible to jealousy, suspicion, fear, anger, and quarrels within romantic relationships. He seeks friendship as a form of soothing and comfort but corrupts it through his need for validation and shared transgression. He is deeply influenced by maternal authority even while rejecting it consciously; his mother's prayers and warnings persist in his memory despite his intellectual dismissal of them. He gravitates toward mentors and authority figures (the Bishop, wise physicians, eventually Augustine's own spiritual guides) and accepts their counsel when they command respect through demonstrated wisdom. He performs versions of himself for different audiences: the ambitious orator among peers, the deceiver among authority figures, the penitent before God. He experiences profound loneliness despite social engagement, describing a "famine of inward spiritual food" and restlessness that no creature-satisfaction can remedy. His relationships improve markedly when he ceases to demand that others fill the void that only God can fill.

## Conflict with Self

Augustine's core behavioral pattern involves oscillation between self-aggrandizement and self-condemnation. He exaggerates his sins to avoid seeming contemptible, then later genuinely grieves those sins. He takes pride in his wickedness among peers, then experiences shame before God. He pursues empty trifles with his considerable gifts, then recognizes the waste. He is "carried away by stage-plays," experiencing feigned emotions as though they were real, suggesting a fundamental confusion between performance and authenticity. His conversion involves not the elimination of these patterns but their reorientation: his rhetorical gifts, his intensity, his capacity for total commitment—these remain, but they redirect toward God rather than toward human praise. His restlessness persists until it finds its proper object.
